How I work

A process built for
systems that learn

Designing for AI requires a different discipline. The interface is only part of the problem — you're also designing for uncertainty, model behaviour, and the trust people extend to machines.

My process

Five stages. One through-line: the user.

No two projects are identical, but this framework adapts to any scale — from a two-week sprint to a six-month product overhaul.

  1. Step 01

    Discover & Frame

    Before I open Figma I open my notebook. This phase is about understanding who the users are, what they actually need (not what stakeholders assume they need), and where the current experience breaks down. Methods include contextual interviews, diary studies, competitive benchmarking, and — for AI products — model capability audits to understand what the system can realistically do.

  2. Step 02

    Define & Prioritise

    Research generates noise. This stage turns it into signal: clear problem statements, prioritised user needs, and explicit design principles for the project. For AI products I also define the "trust contract" — what the system promises to users, what it cannot guarantee, and how those limits should be communicated.

  3. Step 03

    Explore & Prototype

    I sketch wide before going deep — low-fidelity explorations that challenge assumptions without investing in pixels. Once the right direction emerges I build interactive prototypes at the fidelity needed for meaningful testing. For conversational and voice interfaces I prototype the dialogue before the UI.

  4. Step 04

    Test & Learn

    Testing is not a gate at the end — it's woven into every stage. Moderated usability sessions, unmoderated remote tests, and A/B experiments all serve different questions. For AI interfaces I pay particular attention to how users calibrate trust over repeated interactions, not just the first session.

  5. Step 05

    Ship & Evolve

    Launch is the beginning, not the end. I work closely with engineering through handoff, write thorough design specs, and stay close to the product post-launch to catch edge cases that only real usage surfaces. AI products in particular need ongoing design attention as model behaviour changes.

My north star

Principles for AI experience design

  • 🔍

    Explain, don't just show

    AI outputs without context are just assertions. Good design makes the reasoning visible enough that users can evaluate it.

  • 🎛

    Give control back

    Every AI action should have a clear undo, override, or feedback mechanism. Helpfulness without agency is paternalism.

  • ⚠️

    Design for failure

    Models get things wrong. The question is whether the interface helps users catch and recover from errors — or hides them.

  • ♿

    Accessibility is non-negotiable

    AI tools that are inaccessible to users with disabilities amplify existing inequalities. Inclusive design is a requirement, not a nice-to-have.
