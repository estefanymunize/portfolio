How I work

A process built around
understanding people

Good design doesn't happen by accident. It starts with deep curiosity about people, and ends only when something genuinely works for them — not just looks like it should.

My process

Five stages. One through-line: the user.

No two projects are identical, but this framework adapts to any scale — from a two-week sprint to a six-month product overhaul.

  1. Step 01

    Discover & Frame

    Before I open Figma I open my notebook. This phase is about understanding who the users are, what they actually need (not what stakeholders assume they need), and where the current experience breaks down. Methods include contextual interviews, diary studies, surveys, competitive benchmarking, and heuristic evaluations.

  2. Step 02

    Define & Prioritise

    Research generates noise. This stage turns it into signal: clear problem statements, prioritised user needs, and explicit design principles for the project. How-Might-We questions, affinity mapping, and Jobs-to-be-Done framing all live here.

  3. Step 03

    Explore & Prototype

    I sketch wide before going deep — low-fidelity explorations that challenge assumptions without investing in pixels. Once the right direction emerges I build interactive prototypes in Figma at the fidelity needed for meaningful testing.

  4. Step 04

    Test & Learn

    Testing is not a gate at the end — it's woven into every stage. Moderated usability sessions, unmoderated remote tests, and A/B experiments all serve different questions. I pay particular attention to edge cases and the moments where users hesitate.

  5. Step 05

    Ship & Evolve

    Launch is the beginning, not the end. I work closely with engineering through handoff, write thorough design specs, and stay close to the product post-launch to catch edge cases that only real usage reveals. Good products are never truly finished.

What I believe

Core design principles

  • 🔍

    Research before pixels

    The best-looking interface in the world means nothing if it solves the wrong problem. Understanding the person always comes first.

  • 🎛

    Clarity over cleverness

    Complexity is not sophistication. If a user needs a tooltip, the design has already failed. Simple is harder — and better.

  • 🔁

    Test early, test often

    Assumptions are expensive. Getting something in front of real users — even rough — beats polishing something nobody wants.

  • ♿

    Inclusive by default

    Designing for edge cases — cognitive load, low vision, motor impairments — almost always improves the experience for everyone.
